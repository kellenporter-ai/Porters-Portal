# ISLE Pedagogy Reference

ISLE (Investigative Science Learning Environment) is an inquiry-based pedagogy where students learn physics by engaging in practices that mirror how physicists actually construct knowledge.

**Source:** https://www.islephysics.net/?page_id=83

---

## Core Principles

1. **Learning like physicists:** Students engage in knowledge-generating activities that mimic the actual practices of physics and employ the reasoning tools physicists use when constructing knowledge.
2. **Well-being enhancement:** The learning process itself should contribute positively to student welfare.
3. **Collaborative learning:** Students work in groups to investigate, discuss, and build understanding together.
4. **Multiple representations:** Students learn scientific models and notation through authentic use — diagrams, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions.

---

## The 5 ISLE Phases

### 1. Preparation
**Goal:** Establish foundational understanding. Activate prior knowledge. Make the topic accessible.

**What happens:**
- Students explore prerequisite concepts through structured inquiry
- Teacher establishes accessible observations as entry points
- Students recall and connect to previously learned material

**Teacher role:** Set the stage. Ensure all students have the prerequisite knowledge to engage with the new material.

**Typical activities:**
- Review questions connecting to prior units
- Simple demonstrations or observations that introduce the phenomenon
- Vocabulary introduction
- Reading assignments from the textbook

### 2. Observational Experimentation
**Goal:** Students make direct observations that generate genuine questions about physical phenomena.

**What happens:**
- Students conduct hands-on experiments in small groups
- They collect qualitative or quantitative data
- They identify patterns in their observations
- They articulate what they notice and what questions arise

**Teacher role:** Provide equipment and guiding questions. Let students discover patterns rather than telling them.

**Typical activities:**
- Lab experiments with simple equipment
- Data collection and recording
- "What do you notice?" discussions
- Pattern identification from observations or data

**Key phrases in resources:** "Observe and find a pattern", "Observe", "What do you notice?"

### 3. Model Development
**Goal:** Students devise multiple competing explanations for their observations, creating testable hypotheses.

**What happens:**
- Students propose models (explanations) for the patterns they observed
- Multiple models are encouraged — there should be competing explanations
- Students represent their models using diagrams, equations, and verbal descriptions
- Students relate new concepts to known ones (e.g., rotational quantities to linear quantities)

**Teacher role:** Facilitate discussion. Encourage multiple explanations. Don't reveal the "right" answer yet.

**Typical activities:**
- Whiteboard presentations of group models
- Building mathematical relationships from observations
- Constructing diagrams and representations
- Filling in comparison tables (e.g., linear vs. rotational quantities)

**Key phrases in resources:** "Describe", "Represent and reason", "Reason", "Develop a model", "Fill out the table"

### 4. Hypothesis Testing
**Goal:** Students use hypothetico-deductive reasoning to test and eliminate inadequate models.

**What happens:**
- Students make predictions: "If [model] is correct and I do [experiment], then [predicted outcome] should occur"
- They design or conduct testing experiments
- They compare predictions to actual results
- They evaluate which models are supported and which are falsified

**Teacher role:** Guide students through the prediction-testing cycle. Help them distinguish between observational and testing experiments.

**Typical activities:**
- Prediction exercises based on proposed models
- Testing experiments (different from the original observations)
- Data collection and comparison to predictions
- Analysis of which models survive testing

**Key phrases in resources:** "Test", "Observe and analyze", "Which features support the hypothesis?", "Determine... from the data"

### 5. Application
**Goal:** Students apply validated understanding to novel situations, solving problems and demonstrating mastery.

**What happens:**
- Students solve problems using their tested models
- They apply understanding to new, unfamiliar contexts
- They design experiments using their new knowledge
- They make connections across topics

**Teacher role:** Provide novel problems and scenarios. Assess understanding through application, not memorization.

**Typical activities:**
- Problem-solving exercises
- Novel scenario analysis
- Experiment design tasks
- Conceptual questions in new contexts

**Key phrases in resources:** "Apply", "Solve", "Design an experiment", "Reading exercise" (applying knowledge to textbook material)

## Important Notes

- **Non-linear progression:** Teachers can revisit earlier phases and re-examine assumptions at any point. A lesson doesn't have to move strictly from Phase 1 to Phase 5.
- **Phase flexibility:** Not every lesson needs all 5 phases. A lab-focused lesson might emphasize Phases 2-4. A problem-solving session might focus on Phase 5 with brief Preparation.
- **Observation vs. Testing experiments:** ISLE distinguishes between observational experiments (Phase 2, collecting data to find patterns) and testing experiments (Phase 4, checking predictions from models). This distinction is pedagogically important.
- **PIVOTAL activities:** Some activities in ISLE-aligned resources are marked "PIVOTAL" — these are critical for the conceptual development and should not be skipped or simplified.
